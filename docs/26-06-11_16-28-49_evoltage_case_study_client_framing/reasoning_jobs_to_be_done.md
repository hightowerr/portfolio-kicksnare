# Reasoning: Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)

## Thinking Step 1 — Spot the Struggle

The "customer" here is a potential Kicksnare client — a small business owner (tradesperson, local service provider) visiting kicksnare.digital. They are not hiring the case study for intellectual enrichment. They have a concrete struggle:

**The struggle:** "My website looks bad / isn't getting me customers / I know I need a new one but I don't know who to trust, how much it costs, or whether it's worth the money."

This is a real, repeated episode. The typical trigger is one of:
- A competitor got a nicer website and they noticed
- A customer told them "I almost didn't call because your website looked dodgy"
- They searched for themselves on Google and couldn't find their own business
- They got cold-outreached by Kicksnare with a before/after that made them curious

The key insight: they are NOT struggling with "I need to understand CRO methodology." That is a struggle that belongs to a different customer (an agency peer, a PM, a CRO professional).

## Thinking Step 2 — Map the Circumstances

**Who:** Owner-operator of a local trades/services business. Typically 30-55, male-skewed for trades, manages 1-10 employees. Makes all purchasing decisions personally. No marketing team, no technical staff.

**When:** Usually triggered by a specific event — lost a job to a competitor, embarrassing website moment, seasonal slow period where they have time to think about marketing. Or: received Kicksnare's cold outreach email/DM.

**Where:** Reading the case study on their phone (likely) or laptop, probably in the evening or during a break. Attention span: 2-3 minutes max, not the 7-10 minute read the Kickdrum template targets.

**Constraints:** Budget-conscious (£500 is significant spend for a sole trader). Time-poor. Skeptical of agencies — many have been burned by SEO/marketing people who took money and delivered nothing. Cannot evaluate technical claims (they don't know what "JSON-LD schema" means and don't care).

## Thinking Step 3 — Elicit Desired Progress

The client's Job Story:

> **When** I realize my website is embarrassing or isn't bringing me work, **I want to** find someone who understands my business and can build me a professional website quickly and affordably, **so I can** get more phone calls, look credible to potential customers, and stop worrying about my online presence.

The desired progress has three dimensions:
- **Functional:** More phone calls, more enquiries, a website that works on mobile, shows up on Google
- **Emotional:** Pride in their online presence (not embarrassment), confidence that they chose well, relief that it's handled
- **Social:** Looking professional to customers, keeping up with competitors, being taken seriously

**Critical insight:** NONE of these dimensions mention methodology. The functional progress is "more calls" not "lower FR ratio." The emotional progress is "pride" not "appreciation of diagnostic rigour." The social progress is "looking professional" not "having a data-backed intervention strategy."

## Thinking Step 4 — Analyze the Four Forces

### Push (away from current situation)
- "My website looks like it was made in 2015"
- "Customers have told me they almost didn't call"
- "I can't find myself on Google"
- "My competitor's site looks way better than mine"
- "I'm embarrassed to give people my URL"

**Current case study effectiveness on Push: MODERATE.** The "What was costing jobs" section does address some of these — the "Read more..." CTA problem, the buried phone number. But it's framed in diagnostic language ("Friction-Reward ratio 1.80") rather than client language ("customers couldn't find the phone number").

### Pull (toward new solution — Kicksnare)
- "They showed me exactly what my site could look like"
- "It only takes 3 days and costs £500"
- "They did this for someone just like me"
- "The before/after is dramatic and the new site looks professional"
- "They explain things in plain English"

**Current case study effectiveness on Pull: WEAK.** The template mapping has the right data (3 days, 13 pages, before/after) but buries it in methodology. The pull should be VISUAL and IMMEDIATE — the before/after screenshot should dominate the page, not be one element among seven sections. The Rooks Agency case study (comparable: moving company, local trades) leads with simple challenges in plain language and ends with 4 big result numbers. No methodology exposition at all.

### Anxiety (about switching)
- "Will it actually get me more customers or just look nice?"
- "I've been burned by marketing people before"
- "I don't understand what they're offering — is this a scam?"
- "What if I pay and they disappear?"
- "This seems too good/fast to be true"

**Current case study effectiveness on Anxiety: POOR.** The methodology-heavy framing actually INCREASES anxiety because:
1. Jargon signals "this person speaks a different language than me" → distrust
2. No testimonial from a real client → no social proof
3. "Unsolicited redesign" framing is honest but sounds like "I did this without being asked" which can read as desperate or presumptuous
4. The evidence plan section ("what we WOULD measure") admits results don't exist → undermines confidence

### Inertia (habits keeping them stuck)
- "My current website is fine, it's worked so far"
- "I get most of my work through word of mouth anyway"
- "Dealing with website stuff is a hassle I don't want"
- "Last time I tried to change my website it was a nightmare"

**Current case study effectiveness on Inertia: MODERATE.** The diagnostic scores (SEO 10/100, 5-Second Test 15/40) are good at breaking inertia IF translated to client language. "Your website scores 10 out of 100 on Google's SEO test" breaks inertia. "FR ratio 1.80 exceeds the abandonment threshold" does not.

## Thinking Step 5 — Widen the Competitive Set

The case study is not competing against other agency case studies. It is competing against:

1. **Doing nothing** — the most common alternative. "My website is fine."
2. **Asking a nephew/friend** — "My mate's kid does websites for £200"
3. **Wix/Squarespace DIY** — "I'll just do it myself one weekend"
4. **A local web designer** — someone they know personally, face-to-face trust
5. **A Fiverr/Upwork freelancer** — cheap, fast, no methodology claims
6. **Another cold-outreaching agency** — sending similar emails

Against options 2-5, Kicksnare's diagnostic depth IS a differentiator — but only if it's translated into client benefit. "We diagnosed 10 problems with your website before writing a single line of code" is a differentiator. "We applied Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics and Speero's value/relevance/clarity/friction/motivation framework" is not.

## Thinking Step 6 — Model the System

**User:** The business owner (reads the case study, decides)
**Buyer:** Same person (pays from business account)
**Influencer:** Their spouse/partner ("does this look legit?"), their accountant ("is this a good spend?"), their trade network ("have you heard of these people?")

The influencer layer matters: the case study might get forwarded to a spouse for a second opinion. It must pass the "does this look legit?" test for someone with ZERO context about web design.

## Thinking Step 7 — Prototype Around Moments

The key moment of struggle for the case study reader is the first 10 seconds:
- Do I see a business like mine?
- Do I see a dramatic before/after?
- Do I understand what happened?
- Do I see what it cost and how long it took?

If those four questions are answered in 10 seconds, the reader progresses. If they encounter "Friction-Reward ratio 1.80" or "ICE-scored top 3 interventions" in those first 10 seconds, they bounce.

## Key JTBD Findings

1. **The case study's job is NOT "demonstrate methodology."** It is: "Give a small business owner confidence that Kicksnare can make their phone ring more, fast and affordably."

2. **The current framing over-serves logos (reasoning) at the expense of pull (desire) and anxiety-reduction (trust).** The four forces are imbalanced.

3. **The real competitive set is "do nothing" and "ask my nephew."** The case study must beat those alternatives, not impress other agencies.

4. **The first 10 seconds determine everything.** Before/after visual + "3 days, £500" + "for a local electrician like you" = the critical information. Everything else is secondary.

5. **The 7-section Kickdrum template may be structurally wrong for this audience.** A 3-section structure (Problem → What we did → What changed) with big visuals would better serve the job.

## Evidence

- **Reddit r/smallbusiness (2026):** When asked "do you actually read agency case studies?", top response: "A general description on how they approach the problem and what the end result was." Not methodology. Not frameworks. Problem + result.
- **Rooks Agency case study (comparable: Happy Helpers Moving Co):** Structure is Company → Objectives → Challenges → Solution → Results. Challenges described in plain language ("The link to the Phone number is shown only on the home page"). Results as 4 big numbers (+37.5% impressions, +1,360% conversions). Zero mention of heuristic frameworks, ICE scores, or diagnostic methodology. This is a direct competitor in the local trades agency space.
