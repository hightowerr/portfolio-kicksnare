# Reasoning: Aristotle's Rhetorical Triangle

## Thinking Step 1 — Define the Goal

**Action desired:** The reader contacts Kicksnare (DM on X, email, or audit request).
**Belief to create:** "This agency understands businesses like mine and can make my website bring in more customers."
**Understanding to build:** "My website is probably costing me money, and fixing it is fast, affordable, and low-risk."

## Thinking Step 2 — Map the Audience

**Values:** Hard work, fairness, value for money, plain speaking, proof over promises.
**Motivations:** More work/customers, looking professional, not being embarrassed, keeping up with competitors.
**Biases:**
- Skepticism toward marketing/agency people ("they take your money and do nothing")
- Authority bias toward demonstrated work ("show me, don't tell me")
- Social proof bias ("has anyone else used them?")
- Loss aversion ("I'd rather not spend £500 than risk wasting it")
- Status quo bias ("my website is fine, I get work through word of mouth")

## Thinking Step 3 — Audit the Three Appeals

### Current Ethos (Credibility) — WEAK

**What the case study currently offers for credibility:**
- Diagnostic rigour (three independent audits, ICE scoring, competitor benchmarking)
- Technical competence (Next.js, Tailwind, Playwright tests, schema markup)
- Speed claim (3 working days)
- Methodology frameworks (Nielsen, Speero, Kickdrum 7-section template)

**What's missing:**
- ❌ No real client testimonial — JJ hasn't been contacted
- ❌ No "someone like me" signal — no quote from a tradesperson saying "they understood my business"
- ❌ No credibility markers the audience recognises — no Google reviews, no trade association endorsement, no "trusted by X local businesses"
- ❌ No face — who is behind Kicksnare? The case study is faceless. Tradespeople trust people, not brands.
- ❌ The honesty framing ("this is an unsolicited concept") is good ethics but BAD ethos for this audience — it reads as "I did this without being asked" which signals inexperience, not rigour.

**Ethos prescription:**
- Add a personal element — who built this, with what experience? Even one line: "Built by [name], a product manager who's shipped digital products for [X years / for brands like Y]."
- Reframe "unsolicited redesign" as "free audit + concept build" — same thing, but framed as generosity not presumptuousness.
- If possible, get ANY testimonial — even from a peer who reviewed the build, or a user who tested the site. "I showed this to three people and they all said it looked more professional than the competitors" is weak social proof but better than none.
- Add a risk-reducer: "If you don't like it, you don't pay. We'll even show you a free 5-minute video audit of your site before you commit."

### Current Logos (Logic/Reasoning) — STRONG but MISDIRECTED

**What the case study currently offers for logic:**
- SEO score: 10/100 → target 80+ (clear, compelling)
- Pages: 4 → 13 (clear)
- Time to phone: 15-20s → 0s (clear IF translated)
- Schema: 0 → 100% coverage (unclear to audience)
- Content: <300 words → ~7,000 words (unclear why this matters)
- 3 diagnostic methodologies converging (unclear to audience)
- ICE scores (9.3, 8.3, 7.7) (meaningless to audience)
- FR ratio 1.80 (meaningless to audience)
- ~105 Playwright tests (meaningless to audience)

**The problem:** The case study has ABUNDANT logos but it's the WRONG logos for this audience. The data points that matter to a tradesperson:
- ✅ "Your customers couldn't find your phone number" (they understand this)
- ✅ "You showed up nowhere on Google" (they understand this)
- ✅ "3 days, £500" (they understand this)
- ✅ "Before: 4 pages. After: 13 pages." (they understand this)
- ✅ "The phone number is now one tap away on every page" (they understand this)
- ❌ "FR ratio 1.80" (they don't understand this)
- ❌ "ICE score 9.3" (they don't understand this)
- ❌ "105 Playwright tests" (they don't understand this)

**Logos prescription:**
- Keep the data points that translate to client language. Remove the rest.
- Lead with the NUMBER that hits hardest emotionally: "Customers took 15-20 seconds to find the phone number. On the new site, it's always one tap away."
- The 10/100 SEO score is powerful because scores out of 100 are universally understood. Lead with it.
- Replace methodology metrics with outcome metrics. Even predicted outcomes ("designed to increase phone calls by X%") are more accessible than process metrics.

### Current Pathos (Emotion) — VERY WEAK

**What the case study currently offers for emotion:**
- The "Read more..." anecdote — "Nobody in the dark wants to read more" — is genuinely powerful and emotionally resonant. It's the single best line in the entire case study. But it's buried in the Kickdrum S1 Diagnosis section, surrounded by methodology.
- The before/after visual potential is there but not yet captured (Vercel auth blocker).

**What's missing:**
- ❌ No story arc — the case study reads as a technical report, not a narrative. There's no emotional journey.
- ❌ No empathy signal — nothing that says "we understand what it's like to run a small business and worry about getting enough work."
- ❌ No fear/urgency — "your website is costing you jobs RIGHT NOW" is the emotional hook that breaks inertia.
- ❌ No relief/pride — "imagine being proud of your website for the first time" is the emotional pull.
- ❌ No human story — JJ is never quoted, never humanised. There's no "person like me" in the case study.

**Pathos prescription:**
- Lead with the "Read more..." story — it's the emotional hook. "The main button on this electrician's website said 'Read more...' Their customers call at 9pm with no power. Nobody in the dark wants to read more." This is visceral, relatable, and requires zero expertise to understand.
- Add a "what this costs in real jobs" framing: "Every month this website stays live, potential customers land on it, can't find the phone number, and call a competitor instead. How many jobs is that? Even one lost emergency call-out at £200 pays for the entire rebuild."
- Add the pride moment: "What if your website looked as professional as the work you do?"
- If possible, create a mini-story about JJ: "JJ has been an electrician for 10 years. NAPIT certified. 5-star reviews on Checkatrade. But when you search 'electrician Loughborough,' you can't find him."

## Thinking Step 4 — Test Alignment

**Current alignment test:**

| Appeal | Strength | Audience fit | Net effect |
|--------|----------|-------------|------------|
| Ethos | Weak (no testimonial, no face, "unsolicited" framing) | Audience needs HIGH ethos (burned by agencies before) | ❌ Trust deficit |
| Logos | Strong but misdirected (expert metrics, not client metrics) | Audience needs SIMPLE logos (scores, before/after, price) | ⚠️ Confuses rather than convinces |
| Pathos | Very weak (one good line buried in methodology) | Audience decides on emotion, justifies with logic | ❌ No emotional pull |

**The case study is logos-heavy but audience-misaligned, ethos-deficient, and pathos-starved.** This is exactly the signature of curse-of-knowledge-driven communication: the expert leads with their strength (analytical depth) rather than the audience's need (emotional trust + simple proof).

**Target alignment:**

| Appeal | Target | How to achieve |
|--------|--------|---------------|
| Ethos | MEDIUM-HIGH | Personal attribution, reframe "unsolicited" as "free audit", add any social proof available, risk-reducer CTA |
| Logos | MEDIUM | Keep 3-4 universally understood metrics (SEO score, pages, time-to-phone, before/after screenshots). Remove all expert terminology |
| Pathos | HIGH | Lead with the "Read more..." story. Add lost-job framing. Add pride moment. Humanise JJ |

## Thinking Step 5 — Refine Delivery

**Structural recommendation:**

Instead of the 7-section Kickdrum template, a 4-section structure serves this audience better:

| Section | Appeal | Content |
|---------|--------|---------|
| 1. The Problem (1 paragraph + before screenshot) | Pathos + Logos | "Read more..." story + "10/100 SEO score" + "15 seconds to find the phone number." Emotional, visual, simple. |
| 2. What We Did (bullet list + after screenshot) | Logos + Ethos | 5-6 plain-English bullets. "Added a call button on every page." "Built 7 new service pages." "Made it work perfectly on phones." Side-by-side before/after. |
| 3. The Numbers (3-4 big metrics) | Logos | SEO 10→80+, Pages 4→13, Time to phone 15s→0s, plus Lighthouse score if measured. Keep these LARGE and visual. |
| 4. What's Next (CTA) | Ethos + Pathos | "Want to see what your website is costing you? Drop your URL and we'll send you a free 5-minute video audit within 24 hours." Risk-free, generous, actionable. |

The diagnostic depth (ICE scoring, FR ratios, hypothesis documentation) lives on a separate "Our Process" page for the secondary audience (agency peers, sophisticated buyers) — not in the primary case study.

## Key Rhetorical Triangle Findings

1. **The case study is catastrophically imbalanced: ~80% logos (misdirected), ~15% ethos (weak), ~5% pathos (nearly absent).** For this audience, the target is ~40% pathos, ~35% logos (simplified), ~25% ethos.

2. **The single best line in the entire case study — "Nobody in the dark wants to read more" — is the emotional hook that should OPEN the page, not be buried in section 1 of 7.**

3. **Ethos requires a human face, a risk-reducer, and reframing "unsolicited" as "free audit."** The diagnostic methodology IS credibility, but only when compressed to one sentence: "We diagnosed 10 problems before writing a line of code."

4. **The 7-section template serves a peer audience. A 4-section structure (Problem → Solution → Numbers → CTA) serves the actual target audience.** The methodology detail should live on a separate page.

5. **The lost-job framing is the most powerful pathos move available without live results:** "Every month this website stays live, potential customers call a competitor instead. Even one lost emergency call-out at £200 pays for the entire rebuild." This is concrete, relatable, and creates urgency.
