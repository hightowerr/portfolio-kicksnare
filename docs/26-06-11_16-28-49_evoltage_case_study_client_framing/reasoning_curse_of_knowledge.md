# Reasoning: Curse of Knowledge

## Thinking Step 1 — Gauge Prior Knowledge

**The expert (case study author):** Deep knowledge of CRO methodology (Nielsen's 10, Speero's 5 dimensions, ICE/PIE/PXL scoring), SEO auditing (schema markup, JSON-LD, E-E-A-T, Core Web Vitals), frontend engineering (Next.js, Tailwind v4, Playwright testing, container queries), and product management frameworks (hypothesis-driven development, evidence plans, A/B testing).

**The audience (potential client — local tradesperson):** Knows their business, knows their customers, knows their website "doesn't look great." Does NOT know:
- What CRO stands for (Conversion Rate Optimization)
- What a "heuristic" is
- What SEO means beyond a vague sense of "Google ranking"
- What schema markup, JSON-LD, or structured data are
- What Lighthouse, Playwright, or Core Web Vitals measure
- What ICE scoring, FR ratios, or 5-Second Tests are
- What "compose-first Tailwind v4" means or why it matters
- What an "evidence plan" is or why it substitutes for results

**The knowledge gap is enormous.** The author has ~5 years of professional expertise in these domains. The reader has ~0. The Grandma Test: would a grandma understand this case study? Not even close.

## Thinking Step 2 — Define the Goal and Scope

**Goal:** Get the reader to contact Kicksnare (DM, email, audit request).
**Scope:** Show that Kicksnare understands the reader's problem and can fix it.

The current case study conflates two different goals:
1. **Demonstrate capability** (the actual goal) — "we can make your website better"
2. **Demonstrate process** (the author's instinct) — "here's HOW we make websites better"

The curse of knowledge drives the author toward goal #2 because the process IS the impressive part — to the author. To the reader, the process is noise. They want to see goal #1: the transformation.

## Thinking Step 3 — Establish Common Ground

The current case study starts from the author's frame: diagnostic methodology, heuristic frameworks, scoring systems. This is starting from the expert's world, not the reader's world.

**Common ground with a tradesperson:**
- "Your website is your shopfront — but online"
- "When someone searches 'electrician near me,' can they find you?"
- "When they land on your site, can they call you in one tap?"
- "Do they see proof you're qualified and trustworthy?"

These are concepts every business owner understands without explanation. The bridge from here to the diagnostic findings should be: "We checked, and here's what we found" — NOT "We applied Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics and Speero's value/relevance/clarity/friction/motivation framework."

## Thinking Step 5 — Pace and Signal

The current template mapping follows the expert's natural pacing: diagnosis → hypothesis → intervention → build → evidence. This is the logical order for the author. It is NOT the natural order for the reader.

**The reader's natural pacing:**
1. **"Is this for someone like me?"** → See a business like theirs (electrician, tradesperson, local)
2. **"What was wrong?"** → See the problem in language they understand
3. **"What does the fix look like?"** → See the before/after visual
4. **"How much and how long?"** → See the price and timeline
5. **"Can I trust these people?"** → See proof (testimonial, credentials, real results)
6. **"What do I do next?"** → See a clear CTA

The current 7-section template front-loads steps the reader doesn't need (#1 Honest Framing Strip, detailed methodology cards, hypothesis documentation) and buries steps they do need (before/after visual, price, timeline, CTA).

## Thinking Step 6 — Show, Don't Tour Your Analysis

This is the single most important thinking step for this problem.

**Current approach:** The case study TOURS the analysis. It walks the reader through the entire diagnostic process: "First we did a CRO audit, then we did an SEO audit, then we did a 5-Second Test, then we formed three hypotheses, then we ICE-scored them, then we built the interventions, then we wrote an evidence plan..."

**What the reader wants:** The GOLD — the transformation. Show the before. Show the after. State what changed. That's it.

The analysis is the work that happened backstage. The reader should see the show, not the rehearsal. The diagnostic depth is real and valuable — but it should be IMPLIED by the quality of the result, not EXPLAINED in the narrative.

**Analogy:** When you hire an electrician (the very audience!), you don't want them to explain the wiring diagram, the load calculations, or the BS 7671 regulations they followed. You want to see the lights work, know it's safe, and get the certificate. The competence is proved by the result, not by narrating the method.

This analogy is powerful because it mirrors the audience's own business: Evoltage's customers don't care about NAPIT methodology — they care that the fuse board works. Kicksnare's customers don't care about CRO methodology — they care that the website gets them calls.

## Thinking Step 7 — Check Comprehension

No live audience test has been done. But proxy indicators suggest the current framing would fail comprehension:
- The user's own instinct flagged the problem ("I am not sure they are interested in the framing outlined")
- The Reddit evidence says small business owners want "a general description on how they approach the problem and what the end result was" — not methodology deep-dives
- The Rooks Agency comparable uses zero expert terminology in a case study for a moving company

## Thinking Step 8 — Iterate with Feedback

The case study should be piloted with an actual small business owner — ideally a tradesperson who isn't a Kicksnare prospect. Show them the current draft and the rewritten version. Ask: "Which one makes you want to call?" and "Was there anything you didn't understand?"

## Key Curse of Knowledge Findings

1. **At least 15 expert terms in the current template mapping are meaningless to the target audience.** Many should be omitted entirely, not translated.

2. **The case study tours the analysis instead of showing the gold.** The backstage process (diagnostics, hypotheses, ICE scoring) should be invisible. The audience should see the transformation.

3. **The author's natural pacing (diagnosis → hypothesis → build → evidence) is the expert's logic, not the reader's logic.** The reader's order is: someone like me → what was wrong → what it looks like now → how much/how long → can I trust them → what do I do.

4. **The electrician analogy is the killer reframe:** Evoltage's customers don't want a wiring diagram. They want the lights to work. Kicksnare's customers don't want a CRO methodology walkthrough. They want more phone calls.

5. **The depth of analysis is the differentiator — but it should be implied, not explained.** The quality of the result proves the methodology. A line like "We diagnosed 10 problems before writing a single line of code" communicates rigour without requiring the reader to understand ICE scoring.
